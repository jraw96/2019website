Top 3 Books

These are my favourite books of all time so far. They stand out deeply to me because I can still remember clearly the feelings and emotions reading them, months and years after having last read a page from them.

The Count of Monte Christo

Anna Karenina

The Three Muskateers

1. The Count of Monte Christo
Author: Alexandre Dumas
Pages: 1427
Read: 2017
Favourite passage: “By degrees the sun disappeared behind the western horizon; but as though to prove truth of fanciful ideas in heathen mythology, it’s indiscreet rays reappeared on the summit of each wave, seeming to reveal that the god of fire had just enfolded himself in the bosom of Amphitrite, who in vain endeavored to hide her lover beneath her azure mantle.”

The Count of Monte Christo is an epic story of revenge, romance and extroadinary character development. Starting in 1844 Alexandre Dumas wrote and published over 18 chronological installments in magazines and journals, keeping readers engaged with main plots and sub-plots until finally finishing in 1846. When the main story had finally ended and all issues compiled, what was left was a magnificent achievement of story telling and a testament to how plot diversity, woven together at a grand scale over time, can be elegantly resolved in a single ending (I'm looking at you Game of Thrones, HBO...)
With of the 20+ charaters and diverse webs of relations, Dumas remarkably was able to make sure that each character at one point had a pivotal role or significant influence; no perspective failed to leave an impact on the protagonist's journey. Realizing this by the end of the book was incredibly satisfying and brilliantly done by Dumas.
The writing style of Dumas is a constant balancing flow of vivid descriptions and fluid dialogue. Occasionally the book read itself so naturally I felt I could have been in the scene, listening quietly from somewhere. Overall a timeless and genre defining book, and the only story to move me when I realized it's all I'll ever be able to read about of the lives of these characters.
2. Anna Karenina
Author: Leo Tolstoy
Pages: 864
Read: 2018
Favourite passage: “He stepped down, trying not to look long at her, as if she were the sun, yet he saw her, like the sun, even without looking.”

Anna Karenina is a novel about the lives of two people, Anna Karenina and Konstantin Dmitrievich Levin (who became referred to mostly as just Levin), living in 1800's Russia. Both of these characters go through remarkable plots individually, but what made this book leave such an impact on me was witnessing how Tolstoy used the conflicts faced by each character, to weave into and out of the other's life directly and indirectly, and reveal themes and ideas I couldn't see elsewise. While reading in Anna's character circle I was able to ask questions that I could find answers and demonstrations of in Levin's circle, and vice versa while reading in Levin's. I began realizing this more and more throughout the book, until by the end I was mind blown by how much my appreciation of Anna's story was because of Levin and Levin's story because of Anna.
Tolstoy's writing style is already acclaimed and my only contribution to further this is to just commend that the way he can capture ordinary, simple details about emotions, settings or life and reveal how truly remarkable they are is incredible. Tolstoy lets the reader so easily and smoothly slip between the many (there are many) perspectives in the story. At one point there was a chapter from a dog's perspective running in a countryside that I didn't realize until I was back to reading from a person's. My only spoiler of this book (it's pretty much an obsolete spoiler, but stop reading here to avoid it) is to say that it was a good dog.
3. The Three Muskateers
Author: Alexandre Dumas
Pages: 598
Read: 2019
Favourite passage: “In her body and her heart, passion succeeded passion, consuming her ever anew; her life took on the color and movement of clouds that float across the firmament, tinged now with azure, now with fire, and now with the blackness of a tempest which leaves in its wake no trace of aught but devastation and death."

The Three Muskateers is a story about a young man named d'Artagnan from a country-side in France, traveling to Paris to follow his dreams of becoming a Muskateer of the Royal Guard. Very quickly in a series of wholesome and unlikely series of events, d'Artagnan befriends three muskateers (Athos, Porthos and Aramis) and together get swept into a timeless adventure of mystery, romance and sweet, sweet retribution.
The comradory between d'Artagnan and the muskateers stood out exceptionally to me. Dumas described the courage and strength of these characters so clearly that I was able to imagine my friends (in a present day setting) getting caught up in the same endearing and unstoppable momentum originally written for 17th century France. I really do appreciate how the friendship portrayed in 1844, when the story was written, is so accessible and relatable to a reader (me) almost 200 years later. Similarly to the Count of Monte Christo, all supporting and main characters have powerful plot culminations and pay consistent dividends throughout the story for emotionally investing in. The good guys, neutral guys and the brillant minds of their adverseries were truly special to read, understand and see stand up to eachother.
